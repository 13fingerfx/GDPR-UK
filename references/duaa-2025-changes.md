# Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 (DUAA) — What Changed

> Royal Assent 19 June 2025. Phased commencement: the majority of data-protection provisions came into force **5 February 2026**; the remainder completed by June 2026. The ICO confirms the amended regime is in force. Always check commencement status of any specific section on legislation.gov.uk if precision matters.

DUAA **amends** the UK GDPR, DPA 2018 and PECR — it does not replace them. The UK regime remains "UK GDPR + DPA 2018", now as amended. This file is the delta; cite the amended provision, e.g. "UK GDPR Art 22C (inserted by DUAA 2025)".

## 1. New lawful basis: recognised legitimate interests — Art 6(1)(ea) + Annex 1

A new lawful basis for processing necessary for a purpose listed in new **Annex 1 to the UK GDPR**, with **no balancing test** (unlike Art 6(1)(f)):

- national security, public security, defence
- responding to emergencies
- detecting, investigating or preventing crime
- safeguarding vulnerable individuals
- democratic engagement (disclosures by elected representatives etc.)
- disclosures to public bodies that request data to perform their public tasks

Necessity must still be shown, and all other obligations (transparency, minimisation, security) still apply. The Secretary of State can amend Annex 1 by regulations.

## 2. Purpose limitation clarified — new Art 8A + Annex 2

New Art 8A and Annex 2 set out when further processing is **compatible** with the original purpose (e.g. research/archiving, public interest disclosures, legal claims) and when fresh consent is needed. Reduces the previous case-by-case ambiguity around re-use.

## 3. Automated decision-making — Art 22 repealed, Arts 22A–22D inserted

The single biggest divergence from EU GDPR:

- **Art 22A**: defines "significant decision" (produces legal effects or similarly significant effects for the data subject) and "based solely on automated processing" — a decision lacks **meaningful human involvement** where any human role is merely token.
- **Art 22B**: solely-automated significant decisions using **ordinary personal data are permitted** provided the Art 22C safeguards are in place. For **special category data**, the restrictive pre-DUAA position is retained: broadly only with explicit consent, contractual necessity, or legal authorisation, plus safeguards.
- **Art 22C** (mandatory safeguards): (a) provide information about decisions taken; (b) enable the data subject to make representations; (c) enable human intervention; (d) enable the decision to be contested.
- **Art 22D**: regulation-making power to clarify terms (e.g. what counts as meaningful human involvement).

**Practical impact**: credit scoring, recruitment screening, and similar ADM on ordinary personal data no longer needs to fit an exception — but you must build and evidence the Art 22C safeguard mechanisms, and DPIAs remain required for high-risk profiling (Art 35).

## 4. Subject access requests (DSARs)

- Statutory footing for the **"reasonable and proportionate search"** standard — controllers need not conduct limitless searches.
- **"Stop the clock"**: the one-month deadline pauses while the controller awaits clarification it has reasonably requested from the requester.
- Codifies prior case law and ICO practice; the one-month (+2 for complex) deadline structure is unchanged.

## 5. International transfers — the "data protection test"

Adequacy regulations (s.17A DPA 2018) and Art 46 transfer-tool risk assessments now apply a **"not materially lower"** standard: the destination's protection, judged on outcomes, must not be materially lower than the UK's. This replaces the stricter "essentially equivalent" framing and gives the Secretary of State more flexibility for "data bridges". Existing mechanisms (adequacy, IDTA, UK Addendum, BCRs, Art 49 derogations) all continue.

## 6. PECR: cookies and marketing

- **Consent exemptions widened** beyond "strictly necessary": first-party **statistical/analytics** to improve the service, **appearance/functionality** preferences, **security** updates, emergency assistance — with clear information and an opt-out.
- **Soft opt-in extended to charities** for electronic marketing.
- **Fines raised** from £500k to UK GDPR levels: up to **£17.5m / 4% global turnover**.

## 7. Statutory complaints process

Data subjects should complain **to the controller first**; controllers must have a complaints facility, **acknowledge within 30 days**, and respond without undue delay. The ICO may decline complaints where this route has not been used. Build this into privacy notices and internal procedures.

## 8. Children

New duty for providers of information society services likely to be accessed by children to take account of children's **higher protection needs** in service design (reinforcing the ICO Children's Code).

## 9. The regulator: Information Commission

DPA 2018 amendments (DUAA ss.118–119) abolish the corporation sole and create the **Information Commission**, a body corporate with a board; the chair holds the title "Information Commissioner". New duties include having regard to innovation and competition, and a strengthened framework of strategic priorities. Existing guidance, codes and enforcement carry over.

## 10. Research provisions

Consolidated and clarified definitions for **scientific research, historical research and statistical purposes**, including that commercial research can qualify; broad consent to areas of scientific research is expressly permitted where purposes cannot be fully specified upfront.

## 11. Beyond data protection (context)

DUAA also establishes **smart data schemes** (customer data portability across sectors), **digital verification services** (trust framework and register), and the **National Underground Asset Register**. These matter for product planning but are outside core UK GDPR compliance.

## What DUAA did NOT do

- Did **not** replace UK GDPR or DPA 2018 (no "Data Protection Act 2025 regime").
- Did **not** abolish DPOs, DPIAs, or RoPAs (earlier Data Protection and Digital Information Bill proposals to replace these with "senior responsible individuals" and "assessments of high-risk processing" were dropped).
- Did **not** remove the UK representative requirement (Art 27).
- Did **not** change breach notification deadlines, penalty maxima for UK GDPR (already £17.5m/4%), or the age of consent for online services (13).

## EU adequacy note

The EU's adequacy decisions for the UK were extended and then renewed following DUAA's passage — but any UK divergence is a live adequacy consideration. Organisations transferring EU data to the UK should track the European Commission's position; controllers subject to both regimes must comply with the **stricter of the two** where they diverge (notably ADM under Art 22 EU GDPR vs Arts 22A–22D UK GDPR).
