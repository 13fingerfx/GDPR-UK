# DPA 2018 Exemptions — Schedules 2–4 and National Security

> Distilled from the ICO's *Guide to Data Protection — Exemptions* and the Data Protection Act 2018 Schedules 2–4. Exemptions disapply specific UK GDPR provisions **only to the extent necessary**, usually only where applying the provision **"would be likely to prejudice"** the protected purpose. They are applied **case-by-case per request/obligation** — never as a blanket policy. Document every reliance on an exemption (accountability, Art 5(2)).

## How to apply any exemption

1. Identify exactly which UK GDPR provision you want relief from (e.g. Art 15 access, Art 13/14 transparency, Art 5 principles).
2. Find the specific exemption and check it covers **that** provision — each exemption lists the "listed GDPR provisions" it disapplies.
3. Apply the relevant test — usually the **prejudice test**: would complying be *likely to prejudice* the purpose the exemption protects? "Likely" means a real, significant risk, not a mere possibility.
4. Apply it only to the extent and for the time necessary. Comply with everything else (e.g. you may withhold one document from a DSAR but must supply the rest).
5. Record the decision and reasoning.

## Schedule 2 Part 1 — crime, taxation, immigration, legal requirement

| Exemption | Provision | Test / scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Crime and taxation** | Sch 2 para 2 | Disapplies transparency, rights and principles (to the extent they'd require disclosure) where applying them would be likely to prejudice prevention/detection of crime, apprehension/prosecution of offenders, or assessment/collection of tax. Covers both processing *for* those purposes and disclosures *to* bodies pursuing them |
| Risk-assessment systems | Sch 2 para 3 | Fraud/tax risk-assessment systems operated by government |
| **Immigration** | Sch 2 para 4 | Same structure, protecting effective immigration control. (Post-*R (Open Rights Group)* litigation: safeguards were added by regulations — apply with care and current ICO guidance) |
| **Required by law / legal proceedings** | Sch 2 para 5 | Disclosures required by enactment, rule of law or court order; or necessary for legal proceedings, obtaining legal advice, or establishing/exercising/defending legal rights — exemption applies to the extent the listed provisions would prevent the disclosure |

## Schedule 2 Part 2 — restrictions based on Art 23(1): functions of public importance

Paras 6–15, including: parliamentary privilege (para 13), judicial appointments and proceedings (para 14), Crown honours (para 15), and the **regulatory functions** exemptions (paras 7–11) protecting e.g. the FCA/PRA-type "relevant functions", ombudsmen, the Bank of England, protection of the public against dishonesty/malpractice, charity regulation, health and safety. Prejudice test applies.

## Schedule 2 Part 3 — protection of others

- **Para 16**: information about **other people** in a DSAR — you need not disclose third-party information unless the third party consents or it is reasonable to disclose without consent (factors: type of information, duty of confidentiality, steps taken to seek consent, express refusal). Health/social work/education professionals are treated differently as recipients.

## Schedule 2 Part 4 — restrictions on data subject rights

Prejudice-style exemptions from the listed rights for: **management forecasts** (para 20 — e.g. undisclosed redundancy planning), **corporate finance** (para 21), **negotiations** with the data subject (para 22 — e.g. an unrevealed settlement position), **confidential references** (para 24 — given or received, both directions), **exam scripts and marks** (para 25 — scripts exempt entirely; marks have modified timescales).

## Schedule 2 Part 5 — freedom of expression ("special purposes")

- **Para 26 — journalism, academic, artistic and literary purposes**: exempts from the principles (except security), lawful bases, rights, and more, where (a) processing is with a view to publication, (b) the controller reasonably believes publication is in the **public interest**, and (c) reasonably believes compliance would be **incompatible** with the special purposes. The most powerful exemption in the Act; ICO's journalism code applies.

## Schedule 2 Part 6 — research and archiving

- **Para 27 — scientific/historical research and statistics**: exempts from rights of access, rectification, restriction and objection (Arts 15, 16, 18, 21) where the safeguards of **DPA 2018 s.19** / Art 89 are met (no measures/decisions about particular individuals; no substantial damage/distress; results not published in identifying form). DUAA clarified research definitions — commercial research can qualify.
- **Para 28 — archiving in the public interest**: similar structure, adds exemption from Arts 19 and 20.

## Schedule 3 — health, social work, education and child abuse data

Restricts (mainly) **subject access** where disclosure would be likely to cause **serious harm** to the physical or mental health of the data subject or another person (health data — "serious harm test", to be applied via/with the appropriate health professional), or would prejudice carrying out social work. Education data and child abuse data have parallel provisions (child abuse data: Sch 3 Part 4 — exemption where access would not be in the best interests of the data subject).

## Schedule 4 — Art 23-style restrictions

Human fertilisation and embryology information; adoption records; statements of special educational needs; parental order records.

## National security and defence — DPA 2018 ss.26–28

Exemption from most of the UK GDPR where **required for the purpose of safeguarding national security or for defence purposes**. A Minister may sign a **national security certificate** (s.27) as conclusive evidence; certificates are appealable to the Upper Tribunal. Use is narrow and largely governmental.

## Law enforcement and intelligence regimes

Processing by **competent authorities for law-enforcement purposes** falls under **DPA 2018 Part 3** (not UK GDPR) with its own principles (ss.35–40), rights (ss.43–54) and exemption structure. **Intelligence services** processing falls under **Part 4**. If a client is a police force, prosecutor, or similar, route the analysis through Part 3, not the UK GDPR provisions.

## Common misapplications to flag in reviews

- Blanket refusal of DSARs citing "crime prevention" without a per-request prejudice assessment.
- Treating the third-party-data provision (Sch 2 para 16) as an excuse to withhold entire documents rather than redacting.
- Claiming the journalism exemption for marketing or reputation-management content.
- Relying on the research exemption without the s.19 safeguards in place.
- Using exemptions to avoid transparency obligations entirely rather than to the minimum extent necessary.
